4/7/20

Resilience and Facing Down the Unknown,  by Jared Scott

With the coronavirus pandemic slamming road season to a halt, and no way of knowing when we can race again or even train in groups, maybe food poisoning the night before 2020 Cyclocross World Championships was a needed lesson. Yeah, I finished dead last after my strongest cross season and a legit chance at top 10. But given that I had to roll up to the line with an empty tank, unsure if I could suffer through even one lap, meant I ultimately succeeded. I showed up with what I had to give no matter how embarrassingly little, pushed through each punishing obstacle beyond what I thought possible, and honored my commitment to those who believed and invested in me.

This abrupt lesson in resilience came knocking at my door at the worst possible moment. Here was my final CX race ever as a junior—what I’d been working towards all year, if not since my first National Championships at 12 years old in Boulder, Colorado. At 2019 Cyclocross Worlds in Denmark, I learned exactly what I needed to improve and went to work. I focused on how to achieve better results, such as quicker starts, where in past races I’d always put myself at a huge disadvantage those first few minutes. I quit high school mountain biking to focus fully on cross and did strength training twice a week to increase my power bursts, plus countless solo outdoor sessions just working on clipping in.

After multiple UCI podiums nationwide, I felt ready for my first championship win at 2019 Pan-Americans in Canada. Instead I missed my pedal at the start and then crashed at the first corner leaving me fighting from last to 4th place. I worked harder for a solid start at a steep, muddy Nationals in Washington, finishing 2nd. I made the Team USA European blocks, meaning a whole different crazy-fast competitive level, and I struggled to adapt. But I improved each race, with my strongest results yet at Hoogerheide, Netherlands, just a week out from Worlds. Confident, I felt ready to showcase all my hard work and training. But you know how Cyclocross is all about obstacles, and a sudden one took me out the night before THE BIG DAY in the form of nausea, vomiting and diarrhea when I was supposed to be hydrating, fueling and resting.

My coach, Pete Webber, national team director, Jesse Anthony, and my parents all encouraged me to line up as if to race, knowing I could pull the plug any time. The course turned out super muddy, just a grueling power grind with some slick technical corners, normally perfect conditions for me, but now impossible with nothing in my legs. I warmed up, stayed focused, and shoved out of my mind how bad I felt. The light turned green, and we were off. I clipped in perfectly with an explosive sprint from my third-row call up. I made top 10 in the hole shot—my best start ever in a European race. I went as hard as my body let me, but reality hit, and I fell back fast. I never gave up though and ended up the last rider able to finish all four laps. Though crushed, it made me stronger to rise to these uncertain times today.

My whole junior cycling career has been an exercise in resilience, including two broken collarbones interrupting big road and cross goals at 13 and 15 years old. Devastating that a single night hugging the toilet kept me from truly racing CX Worlds last February, on a course suited to my mix of strengths after an intense year of working on my weaknesses. It helped ease the pain some knowing I was on track to return to Switzerland this September for Road Worlds, only this time striving for the top step.

But now who knows when or if the 2020 road season will happen? We’re all learning to scale the same ginormous obstacle, facing down the unknown of a global pandemic. Right now, we all must focus on health, safety and emotional connection in this new reality of social distancing. Right now, we all must push forward even when motivations and ambitions can disappear with each announcement of cancelled opportunities. For me and many other competitive athletes, we must dig deep to keep training hard and pushing our limits to improve though no known races on the horizon.

Guess I’m lucky that tough breaks taught me to be resilient and push through, even when each day might bring more knocks, uncertainty and lost hope. The best we could do right now is tap into what drives us and keep big dreams alive. And more than anything, work to inspire younger juniors to keep the future of U.S. cycling alive. Because we’re all in this together, and must be ready when the time comes to line up at the start once again.

2/18/20

CX World Championships, by Madigan Munro

This year I was lucky enough, along with 5 other junior women, to compete in the first ever 17-18 women Cyclocross World Championships in Dubendorf, Switzerland. No doubt cycling still has a long way to go in achieving equality throughout the sport, however this was a great step forward for junior women and I was incredibly proud to have been a part of history. 

Leading up to Worlds I had been training very hard with my teammates and coaches from Boulder Junior Cycling. I owe so much of my growth and development as a rider to this team. Our coaches would set up mock races with the whole team to stimulate the races we would be competing in and we would have skills sessions to work on tough obstacles or weaknesses we felt we needed to improve for race day. Even athletes who were supposed to be on rest break would come out for the weekend and train with me and three other of my BJC teammates who were also qualified for Worlds, pushing us and supporting us the whole time. The team atmosphere was something I will always remember, constantly motivating me and reminding me why I love this sport so much.

I flew to Europe about a week and a half early to race the Hoogerheide World Cup to help me get ready for Worlds. The course was a lot different from when I raced there last year, it was dry and super fast. Even though I love racing in the mud, it was a great race to open up before heading to Switzerland. Soon after Hoogerheide, we made the drive to Switzerland, where most of the team stayed together in the same hotel. Having gone on the Christmas trip previous to the Worlds trip with most of the Team USA juniors and U23 riders, I had made some new friends and gotten to know the other riders well. It was a great group of kids, and I had so much fun spending time with them. We would ride downtown to try new coffee shops or play lots of card games! One of the things I found really interesting about Switzerland and Europe in general was the train system. It was so easy to walk down to the train station from our hotel and ride it almost anywhere in the city. Not to mention the view of the Alps from our hotel was beautiful!

Race day came around and of course all the junior girls were nervous but so excited to have the chance at making history. We were the first race of the weekend, and although it was sunny and dry, the grass was very heavy and thick, making the course pretty tiring. The race was tough and I didn’t have the best start but I managed to make up some ground in the first few laps and rode my way into third place. Crossing the line was a special moment, seeing the many fans and photographers, family members and coaches, and being able to celebrate with many of the people who helped me get to where I am today in the sport. After this trip there are still many areas I am striving to improve in and new goals I have set for myself for the seasons to come. I am beyond grateful to the many people and organizations behind the scenes who have provided immense amount of support and guidance to allow me to continuously pursue my passion and take advantage of new opportunities, especially BRAC and the MudFund.

12/25/19

Winter Block CX Trip in Europe, by Jared Scott

Belgium’s famously brutal Namur World Cup humbled me hard but its lessons set me up for me to have my best performance yet this World Championships in Switzerland. After a mid-pack finish at Worlds in Denmark last year, I zeroed in on the work needed to improve my results. So I had a stronger season and silver at Nationals. But competition in Europe is just insane and a bit of a shock to the system. Make one mistake and you won't just lose one position or two, but ten. It’s a different style of racing and you got to be super tough mentally as well as physically. 

My teammates and I were fatigued and stale from the long flight over, and Namur greeted us like a slap in the face. Multiple huge drops meant you had to choose just right or else crash. Massive muddy run ups, huge cobbled climbs, a technical downhill sand section over stairs and the notorious Namur off camber meant no let up. [See the photo] The preride was intimidating, but we worked through the sections with team manager Jesse Anthony and I felt ready. Still the aggressive, high-level intensity from the gun caught me off guard and I wound up mid pack at the hole shot, getting caught behind crash after crash. 

After the first couple of laps I started to feel better and moved up until a lap to go when I dropped my chain and struggled to get it back on. Eventually I finished a disappointing 30th place. After soaking in what happened, I saw the race more as a success than a failure. Though I may not have gotten anywhere near my expectations, I realized how essential to fight for that one position always, no matter where you are in the group because it could decide whether you get caught behind the next crash or not. I also learned to always look for openings. Mishaps, crashes and bottlenecks are part of the game, and all you can do to avoid failure is give your best moment to moment, ever ready to respond whether by taking the outside line or running a certain section. I took this all in for races to come. 

Some teammates got sick from the treacherous conditions at Namur and I caught what they had. Just a little head cold, but I woke up in the middle of the night before Zolder World Cup with a sore throat. This definitely messed with my head but I knew I had to still do my best. Nothing will ever be perfect about a race so it’s critical to keep moving forward. Zolder suited me with a good mix of strength and technical -- long straights that I could use my road conditioning to power down but also difficult descents to finesse. I wasn’t going to let a little setback derail my whole race. I took lessons from Namur about the starts and lined up on the left side since the first corner was a left hander. Everyone shifted right and I moved from third row to top 15. Then whether mental or physical, my legs felt heavy and I dropped back hard until lap two when something clicked. I started to gain ground and was around 20th when I flatted. Turns out I ran over a number pin on course far away from the pits and had to ride the flat for a third of a lap. I lost several positions, but again wouldn’t let that define my race. I kept pushing and finished a solid 26th place. Even with more setbacks and difficulties, I earned a much better result than at Namur. I learned how to better handle the nonstop challenges typical of a European cross race. 

Decided to skip the next race to rest and get fully healthy for the remaining Christmas block. Each race I continued to improve my aggressiveness and mental fortitude, and with it came better results. I finished 23rd under the night lights at Superprestige Diegem and 11th at the classic Grand Prix Sven Nys. Even though I had much higher expectations for myself going into this block, I’m pleased with how I’ve grown as a racer. I know I can do better but did well given all considered. I learned how to race aggressively, always looking for small gaps and ready to run if riders are in front of you. I learned how to be mentally stronger and not let small things define my race. I learned how to always fight for position, no matter where you end up in the group. It’s a totally different world of racing in Europe and is MUCH more difficult, way more challenging. But now I feel fully prepared mentally and physically to go into Hoogerheide and World Championships to make Colorado and all those who have invested in me proud.

MTB World Championships, by Ada Urist

In August I raced my first mountain bike world championships in Mont-Sainte-Anne, Canada. While I did not get the result I had hoped for, I gained valuable experience and pushed my limits. The junior races were on Thursday and we left home the Sunday before so that we would have three days of course practice. This course is arguably the most technical of any cross country world cup course and we knew that we would need multiple days to dial in the lines and get confident riding the rock features. However, my bike was delayed by the airline three times and I only had one and a half practice sessions left by the time it showed up. I made the best use that I could of my course time and although the features were at the very limit of my technical ability, I was able to ride them all by the day before the race. Unfortunately it rained that night and the rocks became very slippery. I could just manage to ride the course when it was dry, but when it was wet, I knew that I would probably be running some of the features. 

My call-up was 42 out of 49 so I was very far back but there were two climbs per lap with good passing room so I knew I just had to be patient and make up time where I could. Everyone had told me that the other racers would be aggressive but there?s a big difference between hearing about it and actually experiencing it. Within the first five minutes another rider cut me off trying to make a risky pass, crashed, and took me down with her. Nearly everyone passed me as I untangled my bike from the course tape and I was close to last going into the first climb. I tried to stay calm and work my way up; I made up several spots and surprised myself by managing to ride more of the first section of technical uphill switchbacks than I expected. Going into the most famous feature, a rock garden called La Beatrice, I felt confident that I would be able to ride it but a big crash in front of me forced me to get off and walk. There was less traffic on the remaining features but the wet rocks were more slippery than I expected and I ended up running the larger features. 

I got pulled in the 80% zone with one lap to go which was frustrating but I also knew that my riding that day was not going to give me an impressive result. I felt great physically and had plenty of energy on the climbs but the further into the race I got, the more of the technical features I had to run because my coordination fell apart when I started getting tired. I was stronger on the climbs than many of the riders around me but I lacked the skills and efficiency to ride fast overall. This was my first race against a world class field, and the only way to practice racing against such a large, skilled, and aggressive group of competitors is to be there. I went into the race hoping to gain experience and finish above my call-up, both of which I accomplished. However, my performance left much to be desired and I?m motivated and looking forward to next season. I hope to do more international races and strengthen my riding on technical world cup style courses; many of the races that I do at home are just about power and technical skills don?t really matter, but racing at the world level is so different and unique. I can go into next season feeling confident that I have the experience of racing the world championships and ready to focus on better racing and results. I am very thankful for Boulder Junior Cycling  and BRAC for helping me get to the world championships, as well as all of the USA Cycling staff who helped us out in Mont-Sainte-Anne. I?m excited to see what next season brings and I?m looking forward to continue challenging myself and improving as a racer.

  • Clipping in
  • Pedaling
  • Speeding up

  • Unclipping

We all got it down fast. Cari, Susan, and the other volunteers continually encouraged everyone, reminding us that it was going to be fun. MTB Racing in Quebec by Ada Urist

I recently had the opportunity to attend my first international race in Quebec, Canada. After racing at Sea Otter Classic in April, I was invited to a USAC development camp involving two races in Mont Tremblant and Baie-Saint-Paul. I had been fighting off a bad cold followed by pneumonia for almost three weeks before the race but I was optimistic and hoping to race my best after some challenging races earlier in the season. There were eight other athletes on the trip and other than one of my Boulder Junior Cycling teammates, I did not know any of the other riders very well. It was a great experience having the opportunity to ride and race with new people on different courses.

The first race in Mont Tremblant was at a ski resort where we stayed in condos within sight of the course. We had two full days to ride the course and prepare for the race which at first seemed like overkill, but I quickly realized that the demanding technical features required much more preparation than the smooth courses at home. I knew that the course would be wet and rooty but there was not an easy way to prepare for this living in Boulder, Colorado where the trails are closed when it rains and the terrain is very rocky. The course began on a long fire road which suited my strong climbing but as soon as we hit the singletrack I was quite far out of my comfort zone. I found myself slipping out on the roots and there were a few sections that I planned to run in the race because I couldn?t ride them consistently. In the race I had a good start and held onto the lead group until the end of the climb but as the descent started I lost the group going through the most wet and rooty section. I slid out and crashed hard in the mud but continued and tried to stay smooth through the technical sections. When we hit the fire road I made up a lot of time and passed a few people who had gotten around me in the trees but my skills started to deteriorate as I got tired and lost coordination. I ended up finishing eighth which was not the result I had been hoping for but it was a valuable learning experience. This race was unlike anything I had done before and the field was much more competitive than my other races this season. The race made me realize that I need to combine more skills and fitness training; I typically do intervals on the road and skills work on easy days so I am not used to maintaining bike handling after hard efforts. This works for smoother Colorado courses but on Canadian singletrack the combination of technical and physical challenge made for a different style of racing.

After Mont Tremblant we went to Mont Sainte Anne and got to ride this year?s world championships course. The technical features were unlike anything I had ridden on a cross country course before; it took me several tries over two days to ride the majority of the course but it was a good confidence boost to finally make it through the intimidating rock gardens. I was impressed by watching the Mont Sainte Anne world cup on TV but the course was even more impressive in person. Riding this course almost felt like a different discipline where line choice was more about making it down in one piece than it was about marginal gains. At first this seemed daunting but it got to be fun over the course of the trip and I really challenged my limits in ways that I don?t typically do on my local trails. 

The second race was at the nordic center in Baie-Saint-Paul. I liked this course much better as it was dry and overall less technical. It was still pretty rooty but there were no features I had to walk and the course was long with plenty of climbing. We had trouble following the course markers on the pre-ride and only ended up riding half of it, so the we were riding the second half of the course blind. Luckily there were no surprise features and I made it through relatively smoothly as I followed other riders over the rocks and roots. The Canadian girls started much faster than I?m used to and I wasn?t in the best position coming into the singletrack but I made up several spots on the climbs and held a good pace on the descents. I ended up playing a game of cat and mouse with a few Canadian girls who I would drop on the climbs and they would catch and pass me when we got to the rooty downhills. I ended up dropping them for good with a big effort on the last lap and I finished seventh after a long and challenging race. I would have liked to place a little higher but I was happy to improve from the last weekend even though this field was more competitive.

This trip was a good exercise in independence as all of the athletes had to buy and cook our own food and deal with a large amount of unstructured time. The mechanics Miguel and Than were awesome and washed and tuned our bikes after every ride, as well as stayed in the tech zone during all of our races in case we had any mechanicals. It was eye opening to see the extremely challenging level of world cup style courses and I learned that my current racing and training will have to change a lot if I want to compete at this level. I am very motivated for the rest of this season and the seasons to come as I want to improve my riding and results. I am grateful to Boulder Junior Cycling, BRAC, and USA Cycling for giving me the opportunity to attend these races, as well as Nate Wilson at Catalyst Coaching for dialing in my training all season. I?m looking forward to much more riding and racing coming up and I?m hoping to continue pushing my limits and improving as a rider and racer.

Women's+ Hill Climb Clinic

We couldn?t have asked for a nicer day. This past Sunday, May 26th, BRAC held its second Women?s+ Clinic. Coach Alison Powers taught us hill climbing techniques to get us up to the top faster. 

Importance of gearing

Just like oatmeal, beds, and gels: there?s a difference between too much and too little of something. Same goes for gearing: too easy or too hard of a gear and you?re likely to go slower. There?s a gear that?ll be just right to move you up the mountain faster. 

The same goes for gearing between seated and standing: too easy of a gear when you stand and you?ll be spinning too fast to move. Shifting up a few gears while standing as you pedal helps to maintain cadence and power and push you forward. One of the exercises Alison instructed us to do was to practice pedaling while seated, shifting gears, and pedaling while standing. 

Importance of positioning

When seated, typically, you?ll keep your hands on the hoods. When standing, you?ll want to move your body forward a little to allow for the saddle to move back and forth behind your legs. You?ll also want to pull with your upper body as you pedal standing. This will end up working your lats. 

We also discussed positioning in a race and where the best lines are on hairpin turns. 

We had made it about halfway up after discussing the different techniques of hill climbing. Then we rode the rest of the way to the top to practice on our own. Alison gave us a quick lesson on descending before we called it a day. 

Our next clinic is Road Racing led by Inspired Training Center on June 23rd. You can find the details on the BRAC calendar or on our Facebook page.

Thank you to Stephen and Marcus of pedal RACING who volunteered!

5/20/19

Women's+ Day Camp

BRAC held our first clinic of our Women?s+ Bike Racing Initiative Series April 20th and had a great turnout of 17 folks. Attendees spanned from triathletes to swimmers to runners. Some had never raced before and some had been racing for decades. Those in attendance received Bobo bars, toasters, mugs, Hydroflasks, colorful totes, stickers, discount coupons, Topo Designs backpack, handlebar bag, fanny pack, and a discount card. Thanks to Leslie Etheridge for rallying up women?s+ racing supporters.

During the first part of the clinic, Alison Powers explained the different types of racing and how to prepare physically and mentally for a race. Lynn Taylor explained how to best pin numbers and to avoid crinkling your number as it will interfere with the light for the picture finish. She explained the rules of racing, and who the USAC/BRAC officials are and what they do.

Everyone took a short break to eat lunch and hang out. The afternoon focused on learning race tactics and implementing them:

Warm-ups

  • Heart rate up without loading the legs - higher cadence good, no grinding
  • Short race - hard and short - warmed up by the time you start
  • Time trial - if its long don?t need to do a long warm-up

Positioning

  • Race smarter, not harder - positioning (not in the back), drafting, teamwork
  • Practice being at the front
  • Climbing - Start the climb in the front - by the time it?s done you?re in the back but still in the group

Race plan

  • Have a goal for the race and think about how that matches your teammates? goals, finish plan

Power meters

  • Learn what the numbers mean and how to use them

Post-race thinking/discussing

  • What can you learn or had done better

Everyone joined together in the parking lot to practice biking drills. They practiced riding close to each other (2-3 at a time, then multiple rows), cornering drills (cones), sighting ahead (cones), body positioning (handlebar tops, hoods, drops), getting off saddle while riding.

They ended the day with a mini ?race? to put their skills to the test. A big thanks to Carmen Ledesma and Stephen Douglas for helping out at the clinic and Alison Powers and Lynn Taylor for their knowledge. We couldn?t have made this such a successful clinic without you!

In the U.S. being top 5 to 8 was fine because fields are small and easy to pass and catch up to leaders once the initial speed slows down a bit. I?m also going to start running more regularly come cross training so I?m better prepared for whatever off-the-bike courses at home and abroad.

I have a full road season ahead of me now filled with Euro racing with USA Cycling and my professional development team LUX. I have different goals on the road but Cyclocross Worlds helped motivate and allow me to achieve beyond expectations. I have learned from my mistakes and still have more learning to do, but I?m ready to perform and show my true potential this road and next cyclocross season.

To start off my 2019 mountain bike season I was invited to compete in the UCI Bear Mountain Canada Cup race with the USA National team. The team brought 8 juniors to the race, 5 girls and 3 boys. The race was in Victoria, BC, I had never been there before, but it was beautiful and the weather was perfect for our race weekend! Now what?

Safety.  We are almost inside 60 days to racing season, and it?s time to get Safety on your mind and in your Club Meetings.  We all need to be talking about this.  

We have some new groupings at crits that will mix up some folks who may never have raced together before.  It pretty much happened across all categories, so we are all in the same boat.  Let?s be safe.  Specifically:

  1. Be predictable in your trajectory.  Relax, no one wants undue risk because of a racer who is riding erratically.
  2. Protect your front wheel.  Don?t overlap it with the person in front of you.  One small move as described in #1 above and several people could be on the deck.  Protect your space.
  3. Be careful when passing lapped riders.  Those that lose contact with the group are fighting their own personal battle, their own personal race.  They earned it, they paid for it.  So, when you come by those racers, give them a lot of warning and let them know where you are and what you?re doing.  Make room for them and pass at a safe distance.

It?s your job to be a Mentor.  Yes, really.  We are bringing lots of new folks into racing who are not as experienced as you are.  That means it is up to the established racers to help educate the new racers on safety.  Don?t be a jerk about it, but when you see something, say something.  Try and help the new people grow in this sport that we all love.
    1. And on that note, be welcoming to newcomers, even if you don?t know them.  Cheer them on, congratulate them afterwards, give them a reason to come back.  You were a 4 once, maybe even a 5.
  5. Attend a clinic.  We put on numerous clinics each year.  Women, men, juniors, everyone can attend a clinic designed to help them get more fun out of racing.  Sign up.  Tell your friends.

1/16/17

I?m convinced that now, more than ever, when we come together as an Association, we can do anything. For years, we have struggled with balancing the needs of an incredibly diverse membership: Racers, Officials, Race Directors, Parents, etc. The list is endless.  Everyone has needs, and everyone?s needs are legitimate.  It?s the balancing of those needs that can be a formidable task. 

 

But we did it.  We made real change, somehow reconciling the needs of all the parties, coming together to find solutions.  For that, I am so very proud of you.  Everyone who tried over the years to make this change deserves our thanks.  Several years ago, I (and several members of the Board at the time) sat on a committee that worked tirelessly for months to fix the race group combinations, and the solution that evolved was incomplete at best.  Then later, when you asked me to serve on the Board, this was one of my agenda items.  We set to work with soliciting input, forming an Advisory Council that had representation from all disciplines and categories, holding many listening meetings over months and months, and then finally wrapping things up with the Town Hall meeting last weekend.  Standing room only at the Library? well done folks! 

But it wasn?t just that, no, we continued (and continue) to listen to all the great feedback we received via dozens of emails, phone calls, and face to face meetings afterwards.  I cannot emphasize how broad the input has been, and how constructive, too.

So, here?s what we have come up with:

 

There?s a ton of rationale that went into this table. Opportunities to race, safety of competition, keeping events interesting, and recognizing that we have riders of widely different abilities (like skiers that stay on the green runs vs tackle the double diamonds, hence my little graphic). There isn?t the time and space to dissect each thought and comment we received, but here are the highlights:

  •  The race day had to be shortened.  We have experienced Category Bloat over time and while I believe this has helped us ?shrink less? than our Nation as far as participation goes, it became unsustainable.  This reduced our starts by 2, the argument could even be made that it was really 2.5. 
    • Race Directors who are limited for time can now have a condensed day and still be Cup, something that Racers have continually showed is important.
    • Race Directors who have more time CAN RUN RACES INDIVIDUALLY!  There?s nothing saying an RD MUST combine races. 
  • Remember, slashes in the table represent 2 distinct scoring systems and prize lists.  1 start, multiple races inside a race. 
  • Changing the Junior groupings to work towards a U19 concept allows those that are looking to upgrade and race in bigger fields to do so. 
  • Keeping our Masters categories in 10 year increments for metabolic reasons.
  • Larger field sizes will make for a more robust, satisfying race experience   
  • This preserves a lot of the race groups that are in existence now, so the shock of changing categories or subsets, or let?s face it, change in general, is lessened. 
  • Safety is a big concern.  We believe that Cat 3?s will mostly race in the SM 3-4, but when they do test the waters in the P1-2-3 they will be absolutely certain that this will be much different than a 3-4 race.  It?s your opportunity as P1-2 men AND women to offer legitimate mentoring to those who are coming up the ranks in your sport.  This can be a growth opportunity.  

 

Will everyone be happy?  No.  But it?s unreasonable to think that everyone will ever be happy.  What we do know is that this meets the needs of our Association now, today.  Will things change again?  Absolutely.  Will we learn from this?  Absolutely.  Will you need to get a little outside of your comfort zone and try something new?  Yes, yes you will.  This is our chance to glue on some new tires, so to speak, and enjoy the grassroots racing that Colorado represents so well.

And if you?re reading this from out of state, get in your car and come race with us, we have so much to offer!

Yours Most Truly,

Douglas C. Gordon, The Entire Board of Directors, and BRAC Staff

Growth and our Sport

You might have noticed a theme that I blog about, and that is encouraging folks to go race.  Make no mistake, this is my blog so I write about things on my mind, and racer numbers are one of those subjects. It?s on the entire BRAC leaderships mind, too, but they don?t have to write a blog. 

Nationally, racing is down 12-13%.  Colorado racing is only down in the single digits, so we are doing things better than the rest of the country. 

At this point, no one has a really good idea as to why numbers are down, but there are no shortages of theories.  A few that seem to have merit:

  1. Bike racing is experiencing the hangover of the post Lance years.  We had record numbers of new people getting into the sport due to his exposure, and now that things are ?different? in that area, folks are less interested in cycling.
  2. People have more options today to do other things.  Hiking, recreational riding, family activities, there?s no shortage of other things to do than bike race.  This plays into my next point. 
  3. Naturally, people come in and out of the sport.  A smaller % of those that try the sport stick to it over the long haul.  You can look at my family for that example.  We started when Susannah got into racing in 2005.  Both my daughters raced the kid?s races, we bought them bikes but after the races got to be more than 1 lap, they lost interest.  That?s ok.  Now, 12 years later, I am the only one still racing in our family (36 races so far in 2016 in case you care).  Erin is at college on an Equestrian team, Meghan is fully engaged in elite lacrosse, and Susannah is off in the horse world.  I think it is common for folks to come in, try the sport, have fun with it for a few years and then fall away.  They?re still riding, but not training and racing like before. 
  4. People have too many choices to race.  They have 2 days each weekend, many mid-week options, and the season goes on forever.  There?s no sense of urgency because there are so many races and the average cyclist only does a handful.  This theory, however, still doesn?t explain why racer days are down.

There are a lot more reasons folks offer up for the decline, so if you have one that isn?t listed above, don?t sweat it.  The reasons are less important than the cure. 

What?s the cure?  We need more new people racing. 

How did you get into racing?  I am willing to bet that the vast majority of you got into the sport through a friend.  We had some exposure and knew the sport existed, but it was another person who got us into going fast on a bike with a number.  They showed us where to find info, they may have even taken us to our first race, they helped us join a club (and let me tell you, clubs are the absolute key to this whole deal).  This is grassroots cycling, the very definition! 

We need to pour some fertilizer on our roots.  We need to invite our friends who don?t race.  We need to meet folks at recreational rides and share with them this amazing sport.  We need to bring our neighbors to a race, enter them up, pin on their number for them and then cheer them on.  If we will do that, we will see growth. 

Clubs need to have significant outreach, too.  It doesn?t help a whole lot for us to cannibalize riders from another team, all that does is stir the pot.  We need to find new riders from outside the racing sphere.  Ever do a charity ride and have some guy or gal ride by you like no tomorrow?  Invite them to race.  Ever see that same rider on the bike path each morning laying down some smack on the commute?  Invite them to a local race.  How about the kids at school?  We have races for everyone, all we need to do is get them out.

So what is BRAC doing?  We are outreaching at events like the Triple Bypass and Veloswap, handing out coupons for free or significantly discounted races.  We need to do even more events like these.  The next phase I?d like to see us enter into is advertising in non-racing channels.  Wouldn?t it be great if we had ads in the back of Bicycling magazine?  How about triathlon channels?  I think the opportunities are there, we need to have a budget to support those efforts.  

How about out of state racers?  Look at Tulsa Tough, for instance.  They did an amazing job of building a local race into a nationwide magnet.  We can do that too!  To help this happen, we are flying in the Race Director from Tulsa Tough to come talk to our Race Directors on how they did it. 

At the end of it all, it comes down to us as individuals working to draw new non-racers into the sport.  We need to work this angle personally, and via clubs, all grassroots.

Luckily the entire peloton moved out of the way on all 4 occasions, but it?s not any kind of an exaggeration to say that someone could have gotten killed.  Pretty serious stuff, so I thank you from the bottom of my heart for keeping us all safe while we race our bikes.  Please just keep your phone in your pocket, keep your head on a swivel and assess the threats before they happen.  We are trusting you with our lives.

4/24/16

Sometimes I get a little unsure as to why I am doing this.  The personal cost is high, there?s a lot of hours spent doing the work of BRAC and it takes away from a whole host of other things...  Like family, work, trying to produce 2 races, manage a team and also deal with my own training.  

But then it happens, and I get reminded all over again why what we are doing is so important. 

I raced the Clasica de Rio Grande this weekend (and the Fed Center for that matter) and on lap 3, we passed a female racer who was on her own, out on the prairie.  She was off the back, with no hope of regaining her peloton, no hope of making the podium.  But guess what?  She was giving it all she had.  Tough reserve, never weakening, she was ?racing? her bike.  I thought that was about as cool as it gets.  As we passed her, I yelled out ?Good job!!!!? and mentioned her team name so she knew I meant her.  I have no idea if she heard me, but I hope she did and I hope it gave her a little wind in her sails.

Then I also talked to a buddy of mine who is having heart issues.  Like the kind that kill you.  He?s just trying to race to give himself an outlet, and to try and hold onto some fitness while he and his Doctor get it all figured out. 

And then another guy I race with / against shared with me his struggles to simply survive a race due to his thyroid. 

It pushed a reset button of sorts for me because we all race for different reasons.  Some race for the pure aggression of the sport, some only race to push themselves and the contest is not outward but inward.  Some strive to be the best in the World, some strive to be better than they were last week and never have even a passing thought of finishing in the top half of a race. 

And all of those people and reasons matter, all are equally important.  And let me tell, you, it?s a daunting task to try and reconcile all of those into a sustainable racing scene.  But reconcile we are doing, and it?s not over.  So next time you see an official, a staff member or a Board member, take the time to talk with them about how you see racing, and see if you can take a glimpse into racing from someone else?s point of view.  BRAC isn?t some nebulous group of people calling the shots from some ivory tower, BRAC is all of us, collectively.

  5. Speaking of safety, the Centerline Rule has gotten a lot of press lately, and you may know the back story but at the risk of repeating it, let?s review:
  • In America, we travel on the right side of the road.  That means oncoming traffic is on the left and if you go head to head with a car, or a semi, or a cattle trailer, you will die.  That thin yellow line is all you have to keep you on this green earth, so it?s kind of important to respect it.
  • Apparently all kinds of Governmental agencies like the State, the County and even the Parks folks think that keeping people alive is a good thing.  As such, they get very upset when we, as racers, do things to increase that risk.  So much so that they are sometimes not too keen to let us run our sport on public lands. 
  • We NEED new venues to keep racing exciting, fun and fresh.  We also NEED to keep our existing venues happy so that we can continue to enjoy this great sport that we all love. 

So all I can say is that it?s a really good thing to be a good steward of the resources we?ve been given.  This past weekend, from my own view, we were pretty darn good and that should make you proud, and it also should be the norm.  And, in full disclosure, I ran over the yellow line a few times up in Weld County, and each time I tapped my brakes, lost a spot and got back over.  The officials have a big job to keep us safe, and I am grateful they give up so much of their time to let us do our little thing.  When you see what they make for a full day?s work, it?s a joke.  I dare say very few of us would work so hard for so little.  Thank you.
