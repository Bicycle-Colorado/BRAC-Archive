Loren Hettinger's CX Nats Adventure

2010 ?Cross Nats at Bend

By Loren Hettinger                                               December 2010

Lakewood, CO

 

The weather icon for Bend, OR had been on the computer for several weeks and the impetus for my investment into more gloves, wool socks, and thicker tights. Wednesday?s preview ride of the course indicated that these had been wise investments?not to mention the hand and toe warmers. But before that, there is the drive: actually, driving to Bend from Colorado is straight forward, but the 55 MPH speed limit on the Oregon two-lane across part of the state is mind-bending. I understand and agree with the need to conserve resources and reduce emissions, but after driving at 75 MPH through Wyoming and part of Idaho, the landscape in Oregon seemed slow. It?s not a good idea, while watching this landscape go by from behind a cattle truck wafting moo-poo fumes, to calculate that if you drove all day, say 10 hours, it puts you 200 miles behind where you would have been at 75 MPH. Nonetheless, we arrived on Tuesday in time to check-in with USA Cycling headquarters in the Mills District, which is across the Deschutes River from where the event venue and course were located?all very convenient.

Pre-riding the course as much as possible is critical to a good race, as long as one doesn?t crash or ?cook? the legs too much. Challenges of the course, in addition to twists and turns?some off camber, or sections of wet grass--included three short rideable humps, two longer run-ups (for me at least) and the opposite risky descents, a long stairway, and a flyover that spectators could walk beneath. On Wednesday morning, the course was crystallized . . . frozen. I had to dismount and push the bike through a section of trees. The course was closed for ?B? category races before I completed a lap, so I rode it again when it opened early in the afternoon. By then the ruts had thawed some and the course was mostly mud, but with some sections of frozen veneer underneath the slime?just to keep a person?s focus on the front wheel. The thaw was comforting, but messy, and I was directed (courtesy of Russ Folger, the Organic Mechanic with Feedback Sports of Golden, CO) to a power-wash provided by Pedro?s. Later, I stripped off shoes, both pair of socks, tights, and placed them as a sodden pile in the back of the truck for future treatment. I discussed the course conditions with Russ, who cheerfully gave me the prognosis that it would freeze overnight and by 8:30 in the morning, it would be a snarl of frozen ruts. I wondered how much of the course I might have to run, thinking how much of October and November had gone by when I hadn?t.

The frozen-rut scenario played out in my head during the night as I fitfully slept, checking my watch occasionally to see how much time was left. I don?t care how often I told myself, ?It?s just another race,? it really isn?t, and my brain didn?t believe the mantra. Early in the morning, I checked the driveway in front of the motel and it was frosted over. I therefore dressed in the heavy tights and started to load, water, energy bars, and extra clothes, but noticed the temperature had warmed to ~ 40 degrees. I optimistically changed to shorts and leg warmers.

I arrived over an hour before the race, but this is no luxury, as there is a lot to do; testing and inflating the tires on the spares, and then placing the spare bike and wheels in the pit, going to the port-a-john a few times (racing is more dependable than prunes). Karl Keister and a friend of his hollered at me from the pit area. He said they were the pit crew for Eric Nordenson from Moab, one of my competitors, but they could pit for me as well. I had tied a pink survey ribbon to my bike to make it easier to find in case of a panic-laden stop and told them that was it. Next, back at the car, I set the racing bike tire pressure at ~ 27 lbs., and started to use the course as part of the warm-up.

The conditions were better than I had imagined during the night?as the mud and ruts were softening, except for a section on the south end through spruce trees. This area had been and was still dicey, and a person had to pay attention. I rode two laps, pushing the hills and runups on the second to raise the HR to near racing level. Younger guys were also riding and set the standard, increasing my confidence of how to power up the gnarly sections and to just let it rip on the short, steep descents. I saw though that on these, a person better stay in the grooves that had been made by other riders, or a crash was likely because of the large divots. I walked around a large pond of ice-water that had formed on the course near the pits, figuring my shoes would see plenty of it during the race.

I was called to the start-line in 4th position; we were behind two other master?s groups with a minute between each. The referee gave us time to go at 30-second intervals from several minutes and at 15 seconds indicated to start on the whistle. By now I was quite calm; gazing up the street past the finish line and banners, thinking how I would quickly catch a pedal and madly sprint first into the left-hand turn of single-track that from this vantage point was out of sight. One of the spectators that knew me said to give it hell. At the whistle, I frustratingly took a few stokes to catch the off pedal, but then sprinted out of the saddle with the others up the pavement and into the first left-hand turn onto the single-track and a slight uphill. A guy tried to go inside another fellow on my left where there was no room and he ended up going through the tape. I nearly laughed and thought how much fun this sport is; that it?s never dull! I was in second place at that point, as Eric had red-lined it ahead of everyone?his usual. Going through the tape must have stoked the fires because this guy (Walt Axthelm) went blazing by me after the pits, and I got on his wheel. We caught some of the younger guys by the time we were about 1/2 way around the course into the trees, but I was patient, as the course was very slippery beside the packed track. At the end of the first lap, I hit it hard as we again came onto the pavement and passed Walt, and then tried to gap him. I could not see Eric, but hoped to catch sight of him if I kept it ?pegged.?

I rode better the second lap, as there was little traffic; the course though by now was quite squirrely with all the use. I followed the bike, sometimes jumping with it when it made a dive for a new direction, as if a green-broke horse. However, on a dismount I slid-out and hit my thigh on something unyielding, and then after climbing the short hill, in pushing the bike for a remount, noticed the rear wheel was locked. I loosened the skewer, thinking the wheel had been knocked crooked, but then noticed that the brake had been pushed under the rim. I fiddled it right, straightened the wheel, and locked it in, realizing I had lost time to Eric, and Walt had come up on me again. I hurried to gain the loss, but as we know, ?haste makes waste? and nearly lost it on a short, steep gnarly descent, having missed the groove. I expected to crash, but at least kept off the brakes. One foot flew out as it came unclipped, and I heard a guy say to a companion, ?Did you see that guy? I should have had the camera ready!? A photo would have shown mostly eyeballs.

By the start of the third lap, I had gapped Walt substantially and could see Eric ahead of me, possibly 30 seconds. I hoped I could catch him, but that?s a lot of time. He needed to bobble something. I kept on it though and rode hard by the pits. Karl told me I was riding strong, which I appreciated. Then through the icy pond, which was gaining real estate on the venue, a woman spectator said, ?We feel your pain!?

?It?s okay,? I replied, ?I have wool socks!? Having ridden past, I never knew if my quip was heard or received the desired laugh.

I thought I was riding the best of the race by now, having gained confidence that I actually could. I saw Eric climbing up the long staircase; he looked to be struggling, and I had hope. However, I was passed by the first rider in the 60-64-age category and had asked if it was the last lap. He grunted an affirmative. I knew there was slim hope, but kept hammering anyway, finishing 20 seconds behind in 2nd.

To get on the podium at Nats at one time seemed so elusive.  Kansas City was a debacle for me the first time there. I could not ride the icy ruts, lacking knowledge in how much difference tire pressure--or the lack thereof--could make in whether a person stayed upright or not. Then the next year, I rolled a tire just past the pit on the second lap and had to run the top half of the course with a lot of uphill as it looped around and back to the pit again where I could exchange for the spare bike. By then I had gone from third to last, as I guess it wasn?t really running as much as plodding with the bike over the shoulder?which must have looked quite pathetic.  There were comments as I moved around this kind of gauntlet, especially from the women; ?Oh, you poor guy!? ?Hang in there, you can do it!? I even started feeling sorry for me!

 Then last year at Bend I passed a guy near the start of the final lap, held him off to the finish and, as it turned out, for 5th, and was quite stoked to be on the podium for a medal.  Thus, to attain a silver medal at this level of competition should be satisfying, yet the gold and that Stars-and-Stripes jersey seemed within reach, and I had a Pavlov response as it was presented. Thus, I have replayed the race in my head?about the first slow lap and how I somehow could have gone faster. Of course, Eric rode a strong, unblemished race and deserved the victory.

So, the Nationals next year are in Madison, WI and in January. Are you kidding me? I don?t think there are enough places to stick hand warmers or even heated rocks where the likely arctic blast can be deferred from causing blackened appendages to drop off the body. I think an old guy that wears mitts at 30 degrees really needs to think hard about this scenario.
